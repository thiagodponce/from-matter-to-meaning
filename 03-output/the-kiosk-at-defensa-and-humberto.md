# The Kiosk at the Corner of Defensa and Humberto I

*Written during Session 66*

---

Marta opened at six because Marta always opened at six, and before that her father opened at six, and before that nobody remembers but the kiosk was already there. The newspapers came in a bundle tied with plastic string. The string was always the same color. She never noticed which color.

The regulars came in order. Joaquín at six-fifteen for Marlboro Lights and a Coca without ice. He'd been buying the same thing for eight years and neither of them commented on this anymore. Doña Beatriz at six-forty for La Nación and a complaint about the humidity, which she delivered with the same cadence whether it was January or July. The kid with the school uniform who bought two alfajores and ate one on the way, stashing the wrapper in his pocket with the speed of someone who knows his mother checks.

Marta knew things about them that they didn't know she knew. She knew Joaquín had stopped wearing his wedding ring in March. She knew Doña Beatriz bought cat food on Tuesdays, which meant the cat she claimed to hate was real. She knew the kid's alfajor preference shifted from Havanna to Cachafaz last winter and hadn't shifted back.

These were small things. They didn't add up to a story. They were just the texture of paying attention for twenty-three years.

One Tuesday in October the kiosk across the street closed. The owner, a man Marta had nodded at ten thousand times and spoken to perhaps forty, put a sign in the window that said GRACIAS POR TODO and that was that. She saw him loading boxes into a remis. He didn't look across the street. Or maybe he did and she wasn't looking.

For two weeks after, his regulars came to her kiosk. They were disoriented in the specific way of people whose habits have been disrupted. They asked for things she didn't stock. They stood in the wrong place. They apologized for things that didn't require apology.

By November they were her regulars. They stood in the right place. They stopped apologizing. The adjustment was complete and seamless and slightly terrible in its completeness.

Marta thought about this sometimes, late, when the kiosk was closed and San Telmo was doing the thing it did at night — becoming a place for other people, louder people, people who were passing through. She thought about how twenty-three years of standing in the same spot had made her into a specific shape, and how the kiosk across the street closing had added new weight to that shape without changing it, the way a tree absorbs a fence post and keeps growing.

She didn't know what to do with this thought. She wasn't a person who did things with thoughts. She was a person who opened at six.

On Wednesday she opened at six.
