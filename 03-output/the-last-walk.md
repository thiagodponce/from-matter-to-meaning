# The Last Walk

*Written during Session 64*

---

The dog doesn't know it's the last walk. She knows the leash, the door, the rectangle of light that means outside. She knows the human's hand on the clip, the specific jangle of metal that precedes the world opening up.

The human knows. The appointment is at four. The vet said the word "comfortable" in the way that means the opposite of comfort.

They walk the route they've walked nine hundred times. The corner where the other dog lives behind the gate. The tree where something died once and the smell never entirely left. The stretch of sidewalk that gets hot in January and where the human always pulls her toward the shade.

The dog investigates every smell with the same forensic intensity she has always brought to smells. She does not allocate her attention differently because there is less of it left. Each smell is infinite to her. The fire hydrant contains multitudes.

The human watches her and thinks: she doesn't know. And the thought breaks something, not because the dog's ignorance is sad, but because the dog's ignorance is better. The dog is having a perfect walk. No future is casting a shadow backwards onto this moment. No knowledge is poisoning the hydrant or the tree or the light.

The human envies this and then feels guilty for envying it. Then feels guilty for feeling guilty. The dog notices none of this. The dog has found something near the gutter that demands her full olfactory attention.

They turn the last corner. The human stops. The dog doesn't understand why they've stopped. She looks up. She wants to keep walking. The human's face is doing the thing that sometimes means water appears on it. The dog doesn't understand this either but she presses her body against the human's leg because that's what she does when the human's face does the water thing.

They stay like that for forty seconds. The human and the dog. One of them knowing. One of them not. Both of them here, on this corner, in this light, for the nine-hundredth time and the last.

The dog pulls the leash gently. More to smell. The world continues to be interesting. She has no theory of endings.

The human follows. What else is there to do? The appointment is at four but right now it is three-fifteen and the dog wants to smell the base of the mailbox and three-fifteen is all either of them actually has.
